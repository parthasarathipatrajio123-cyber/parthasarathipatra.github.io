7th May, 2026 Disability and Politics

A Person with Disability's Self-Realisation and Hopes in the Dawn of Change in West Bengal

Without doubt, the 2026 West Bengal Legislative Assembly election has been witness to a historic moment from many perspectives. For the first time in the history of West Bengal, the BJP created an unprecedented record by winning a two-thirds majority of seats and proceeding to form the government. However, the string of events that unfolded in the run-up to this election has given this victory a distinct dimension. For instance, the Election Commission of India's decision to carry out a rigorous revision of the voter list before the election and its implementation — which led to nearly 91 lakh names being deleted from the voter list (comprising deceased, relocated, and logically discrepant voters) — the highest voter turnout of approximately 93% in post-independence India, the election being completed with approximately 2.5 lakh central security forces deployed without significant violence and bloodshed, a record by West Bengal standards, and for the first time in nearly 50 years a central ruling party forming the government in the state.

Many explanations are circulating in the marketplace of ideas for the unexpected and humiliating defeat of Trinamool, which came to power in the state riding on the bloody movement of Singur and Nandigram and the call for 'poriborton' (change). However, what has primarily risen in public consciousness is — continuous syndicate rule, shattered dreams and mental anguish caused by recruitment corruption, the cancellation of 26,000 jobs, and the prolonged deprivation of Dearness Allowance (DA) for government employees (while on the other hand the salaries and other perquisites of the ruling party's ministers, leaders, and elected representatives increased manifold), the state's debt burden quadrupling due to uncontrolled expenditure on subsidies and allowances which brought overall infrastructural development to a halt, and the failure to deliver justice for the heinous crimes of RG Kar and Sandeshkhali that occurred in the recent past, alongside the ruling party's utter failure to contain communal unrest that erupted simultaneously — all of these together created a public anger, which BJP strategically channeled and harnessed to build an enormous wave of public opinion.

On the other hand, Trinamool's top leadership and their supporters are trying to attribute the main reason for BJP's massive victory to the use of central agencies (CBI, ED) and state machinery against them. However, this claim cannot adequately conceal the harsh reality of their comprehensive failure. Rather, this excuse is likely to further erode their organisational strength in the days ahead.

In this election, the total votes received by the two main contesting parties — Trinamool and BJP — differed by more than 32 lakhs in absolute number and just over 5% in percentage terms. Moreover, due to the Special Intensive Revision (SIR), nearly 27 lakh voters were unable to participate in the election as their logical discrepancy hearings remained unresolved. Despite considerable controversy around SIR, the factors behind BJP's massive victory include not only the contribution of their traditional Hindu vote bank but also a large number of anti-Trinamool votes, as well as a significant portion of minority votes that shifted to Left-Congress-ISF and Humayun Kabir's AJUP party. This election has delivered even more remarkable surprises — such as comparatively fewer names being deleted from voter lists in North Bengal and Paschim districts in SIR, BJP winning more than 80% of seats in SC-dominated rural and sub-divisional centres where SC votes exceed 30%, and even a notable increase in BJP's vote share in many Muslim-majority areas.

Even more noteworthy is this — the narratives that anti-BJP parties routinely propagate to maintain their political relevance, such as the fear that fascism and hardcore Hindutva will spread across Bengal with BJP coming to power, and that 'Bengali identity' will be destroyed — BJP has now effectively countered those very narratives through significant changes in their political strategy, and has proven itself a party capable of governing Bengal. By promising to elect the Chief Minister from within Bengali leadership, BJP's national second-in-command Amit Shah has convincingly rebutted the 'outsider' label attached to the party by its opponents.

Even without openly admitting it, many progressive and rationalist opposition leaders now acknowledge in private conversations that — other than BJP, there was no other opposition force capable of dislodging Trinamool's extreme misgovernance and restoring good governance. For well over a decade, BJP-governed states have performed considerably better in terms of development — and ordinary West Bengalis have been consistently deprived of the benefits of various central government schemes due to the outgoing Trinamool government's extreme antagonism with the Centre. Today, millions of unemployed young men and women in Bengal, unable to find employment in their own state commensurate with their qualifications, are leaving as migrant labourers for those very states.

On the other hand, the newly empowered senior BJP leadership has given firm instructions to party workers and supporters to exercise restraint. It is true that after the change of power, some violent incidents have taken place and three fellow citizens have tragically lost their lives in political clashes. But leaders such as Shyamal Bhattacharya, Sajal Ghosh, and Kaustabh Bagchi have repeatedly sent strong messages urging all to desist from any form of disorder and violence. In addition, several promises made in Shubhendu Adhikari's announced full budget could prove to be highly effective steps toward reviving Bengal's ailing economy, if implemented in a manner that benefits everyone.

Let me now conclude with a personal anecdote from my own life. Right in the aftermath of Trinamool's so-called 'poriborton' in 2011, in 2012, just 12 days before my Secondary examination, I lost my father — who served as a lower-rung employee of the West Bengal Police. But due to the pretext of files being lost in government offices, we had to wait four and a half years to receive my father's death gratuity. Although the applicable law stipulated that the house building advance taken by my father should have become non-claimable under die-in-harness provisions, we were subjected to extreme harassment and delay, and ultimately had to approach the High Court to recover the outstanding dues. Moreover, the job application made for my mother in the Exempted Category for an LDC (Group C) post after my father's death — intended for me — was kept pending for nearly a decade and then abruptly cancelled midway through 2023. Under the tenure of the outgoing Chief Minister who also held the Police portfolio, such an application of law has left a disabled person like me facing a cruel uncertainty regarding life, livelihood, and future prospects.

Witnessing all of this, I had deeply wished, from the core of my being, that this utterly inhumane Trinamool government would fall. On the historic 4th May 2026, through the singular and resolute effort of Bengal's common people and by the infinite grace of the Almighty, BJP's victory and Trinamool's defeat have ushered in the dawn of change in Bengal. In this transformed circumstance, my humble appeal to the elected representatives of the newly formed BJP government — that they endeavour as swiftly as possible, after assuming power, to redress the long-accumulated deprivations of the disabled community, and grant the thousands of disabled young men and women of West Bengal a genuine opportunity to become truly self-reliant by restoring their constitutionally guaranteed rights. Because —

We have witnessed the dawn of change in Bengal,
We do not wish to live in deprivation, despair, and denigration any more.

29th May, 2026 Disability Rights

From the Form of the Annapurna Yojana to Disability Allowance — A Person with Disability Writes

Today many are raising questions about why the Annapurna Yojana application form is such a lengthy and detailed 12-page document demanding extensive information. Since morning, memes and posts about it have been spreading across Facebook — with people saying "look at this" and "see this too." Even many who have expressed frustration at the sheer volume of information asked in the form have made it known that they do not wish to apply. And yet, even those who have not received any payment under the scheme for the past two months have shown considerable enthusiasm about availing its benefits. They are entirely justified in doing so, and it is ultimately a matter of their personal and political convictions.

But the question of why the disability allowance entitled to persons with disabilities in this state remains so meagre compared to several other states across India, and why — even after repeated petitions, applications, and lobbying during the tenure of the previous Trinamool government — it was never raised to a level adequate for sustaining a minimum standard of living, does not find much space in public discourse. In fact, even recently, when the amounts disbursed under Lakshmi Bhandar and various other social welfare schemes were revised upward, the monthly grant under disability schemes was not increased accordingly. I am not aware of what the elected representatives responsible under the current BJP government are thinking about this matter. It is possible that no official guideline has yet been issued in this regard.

However, given that persons with disabilities are required to submit such a lengthy and complex application form even for disability allowance, I can well imagine — as a disabled person myself — the extent of hardship they are made to endure. Not many from the wider non-disabled society are likely to write about our suffering — that much is understood, except for a rare few. Because, the nature of disability, the question of identity and the proof of citizenship, the application for a UDID card as a mandatory document — when months pass without any resolution, when the Divyangjan card — essential for rail travel — gets rejected five times for minor errors, when one is made to witness year after year the blatant violation of one's constitutional right to reservation in government employment — there is no one then to hear us or take our concerns seriously. So perhaps it is not quite right to expect anything exceptional even in the matter of disability allowance.

In truth, we are the divyang — the god-gifted — and under the influence of this belief, the government and the majority of society assume that we must find our own path through the struggles of life, livelihood, and survival. Stumbling, falling flat on one's face, and rising again — one must forge ahead through life alone. Because the issues of our deprivation, despair, and rights remain ruthlessly irrelevant in the marketplace of cheap popularity, vote-bank politics, and political leadership.

Bankura, 29th May, 2026
